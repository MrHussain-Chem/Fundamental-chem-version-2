How to revise — and why it works

You might already be putting time into revision — but if it isn’t showing up in your results, the way you’re revising might need a tweak. Here’s the approach that works.

Step 1 — Recap a little, often (20–30 mins a week)

What to do: Each week, spend a short block recapping chemistry — aim for roughly the same amount of recap time as the lessons you had that week. Two lessons that week? Aim for about an hour of recap across the week.
What to focus on: Look back at your assessments, mock exams and homework booklets and note every area you’re weak in. For most people that’s Year 9 content — and we want to enter Year 11 on solid foundations, with Year 9 and 10 already revised. If you couldn’t answer the starter questions in class, those are weak areas too — start there.
Getting better once isn’t enough. Being good at a topic today doesn’t mean you’ll still be good at it in six months. Revisit content throughout the year. Repetition and practice are what make it stick.

How do I actually recap?

Flashcards for definitions, equations, unit conversions, symbols, and any small question you keep slipping up on. Use them daily until you get the answer word-for-word right 3–4 days in a row, then revisit a week later.
Summary sheet / mind map for broader content: summarise a topic onto a double-sided sheet. If it’s good, it should help you answer the homework booklet. If it doesn’t, fix it using the correct language from the mark scheme. Learn it by reading and writing it out loud 3 times, then writing it from memory 3 times.

Step 2 — Test whether it’s working (10–15 mins a week)

What to do: Spend a short block doing about 15 marks of exam questions on the topics you’ve been recapping. In the two weeks before a test, build this up to around an hour a week. Questions live in your homework booklets and previous in-class assessments.
Still making mistakes? First check your revision resources aren’t wrong. If they’re fine and you’re still slipping up, it’s probably a gap in understanding — bring those exact questions to a lesson or intervention session, or reach out to your chemistry teacher for help and support.

In lessons: focus, ask for help when something doesn’t make sense, answer questions even if you might be wrong, and bring your equipment. Mistakes along the way are completely fine.

Step 2 of 8

Learn the essentials

Read one box at a time. Don’t rush — these are the facts everything else builds on.

The atom. Every substance is made of atoms. An atom has a tiny central nucleus (protons + neutrons), surrounded by electrons in shells (energy levels).

The three particles

ParticleChargeRelative massWhere
Proton+11Nucleus
Neutron01Nucleus
Electron−1Very small (almost 0)Shells
Why is electron mass ignored? An electron’s mass is so tiny (very small / almost zero) compared with a proton or neutron that it makes no real difference to the atom’s mass.
Atomic number = number of protons (bottom number). This is what is always different between different elements.
Mass number = protons + neutrons (top number).
Neutrons = mass number − atomic number.
Electronic structure. Electrons fill inner shells first. Shell 1 holds up to 2, shell 2 up to 8, shell 3 up to 8. Example: sodium (11 electrons) = 2, 8, 1.
Isotopes are atoms of the same element — same number of protons — with a different number of neutrons.
The periodic table. Groups = columns (same number of outer electrons → similar properties). Periods = rows. Arranged by atomic number.

How ions form

An ion is a charged atom. Atoms become ions by losing or gaining electrons to get a full outer shell.
• Metals lose electrons → positive ions.
• Non-metals gain electrons → negative ions.
Charge comes from the group: Group 1 → 1+ · Group 2 → 2+ · Group 6 → 2− · Group 7 → 1−.
Tip: metals’ charge = group number; non-metals’ charge = group number − 8.
Both ways: Sodium 2,8,1 → loses 1 electron → Na⁺ (2,8). Oxygen 2,6 → gains 2 electrons → O²⁻ (2,8).
Careful with ions: a chloride ion (Cl⁻) has 18 electrons — 17 from the atom plus 1 gained.

Ionic bonding (taught now, revisited in Year 10)

Ionic bonding happens between a metal and a non-metal. The metal transfers electrons to the non-metal, making a positive ion and a negative ion. They are held together by the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
Sodium gives its 1 outer electron (✕) to chlorine. Both end with full outer shells. Na becomes 1+, Cl becomes 1−.
Magnesium gives 2 outer electrons (✕✕) to oxygen. Mg becomes 2+, O becomes 2−.
Why do ionic compounds have high melting points? There are strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the oppositely charged ions, and a lot of energy is needed to overcome them.
Conducting electricity: solid ionic compounds don’t conduct, but they do when molten or dissolved in water, because the ions are then free to move.

Group 1 — the alkali metals

Reaction with water: Group 1 metal + water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen.
What you see (the demo): the metal floats and moves around on the surface, fizzes, gets smaller and disappears. Further down the group the reaction is more violent.
Reactivity INCREASES down Group 1. Going down, atoms have more shells, so the outer electron is further from the nucleus, the attraction is weaker, and the electron is more easily lost.

Group 7 — the halogens

Reactivity DECREASES down Group 7. Going down, the outer shell is further from the nucleus, so it’s harder to attract and gain an electron.
Displacement: a more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive one from its compound. E.g. chlorine + sodium bromide → sodium chloride + bromine.
Watch out: the two trends go in opposite directions. The key word in both explanations is “outer” — leave it out and the exam caps your marks.

Step 3 of 8

History of the atom

Our picture of the atom changed as scientists did new experiments. Learn the order and who did what.

1. Dalton — described atoms as tiny solid spheres that couldn’t be divided.
2. Thomson — the plum pudding model. After discovering the electron, Thomson suggested the atom was a ball of positive charge with electrons stuck in it (like fruit in a pudding).
3. Rutherford — the alpha particle scattering experiment. Positive alpha particles were fired at thin gold foil. Most went straight through, but a few were deflected.
• Most passing through → the atom is mostly empty space.
• Some deflected → there is a small, positively charged nucleus where the mass is concentrated.
4. Bohr — showed electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed shells (energy levels) at set distances.
5. Chadwick — discovered the neutron, completing the modern picture of the nucleus.

Exam favourites: identifying the plum pudding model from a picture, why most alpha particles aren’t deflected (atom is mostly empty space), and what scattering showed (mass and positive charge are concentrated in the nucleus).

Exam-style questions

Have a real go first. Stuck? Use the hints — they help you think, they don’t just give the answer.

1. Complete the table for fluorine (atomic number 9, mass number 19). [3 marks]

ParticleNumber
Protons
Neutrons
Electrons
—
🧠 Think first: protons = atomic number.
📋 Next clue: neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 19 − 9.
✅ Last step: a neutral atom has the same number of electrons as protons.

2. What is meant by ‘isotopes’? Refer to subatomic particles. [2 marks]

Show what a strong answer includes

✓ Atoms (of the same element) with the same number of protons.

✓ But a different number of neutrons.

Do NOT write: “different relative atomic mass” — the mark scheme rejects it. “Different mass numbers” is ignored (won’t earn the mark). Stick to protons and neutrons.

3. Explain why an atom has no overall charge. Use the relative charges of subatomic particles. [2 marks]

Show what a strong answer includes

✓ Protons have a charge of +1 and electrons have a charge of −1.

✓ There are equal numbers of protons and electrons, so the charges cancel out / balance.

4. A sodium atom has the structure 2,8,1. Describe how it forms an ion, and give the symbol of that ion. [2 marks]

🧠 Think first: sodium is a metal — do metals lose or gain electrons?
📋 Clue: it has 1 electron in its outer shell — how many would it lose to get a full outer shell?
Show what a strong answer includes

✓ It loses 1 electron (from its outer shell).

✓ Forming a 1+ ion, Na⁺ (structure becomes 2,8).

5. A small piece of lithium is added to water. Describe two things you would observe. [2 marks]

Show what a strong answer includes

✓ Any two from: floats / moves around on the surface / fizzes (bubbles) / gets smaller and disappears / makes a sound.

The gas given off is hydrogen, and a metal hydroxide forms.

6. Explain why sodium chloride has a high melting point. [3 marks]

Show what a strong answer includes

✓ It has a giant ionic lattice / structure of oppositely charged ions.

✓ There are strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the oppositely charged ions.

✓ A lot of energy is needed to overcome / break these forces.

Examiner tip: in “explain” questions you must give a reason, not just a fact — and for melting points, always end with “so a lot of energy is needed to overcome the forces.”

Step 8 of 8 · the hardest one

Longer answer — compare your answer

This is the big one — it’s worth 4 marks and appears on real papers. Write what you can, then reveal and compare.

Explain why the reactivity of Group 1 elements increases going down the group (lithium to rubidium), but the reactivity of Group 7 elements decreases going down the group (fluorine to iodine). [4 marks]

🧠 Think first: going down a group, atoms gain more shells — what happens to the distance from the nucleus to the OUTER shell?
📋 Clue: Group 1 needs to LOSE its outer electron; Group 7 needs to GAIN one. The same change in distance affects these opposite jobs in opposite ways.
Reveal model answer & the mark scheme

The real mark scheme awards one mark for each of these four points:

✓ The outer electron/shell gets further from the nucleus down the group (more shells / larger atom).

✓ So the attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron changes (weaker down the group).

✓ Group 1: the outer electron is more easily lost, so reactivity increases.

✓ Group 7: it is harder to gain an electron, so reactivity decreases (the opposite explanation).

Mark scheme cap: you get a maximum of 3 out of 4 if you don’t use the word “outer”. Always say “outer electron” or “outer shell”.
